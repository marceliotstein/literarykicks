Fyodor Dostoevsky wrote "Notes From Underground" in 1864, in the middle of his highly dramatic life. In the early 1840's he'd been a literary sensation among Russia's liberal/radical intelligentsia, but the shy young man was tormented by a social awkwardness that was probably more visible to him than to anybody else. His confusing early period ended when the government arrested him and several other young free-thinkers for alleged revolutionary activities. The entire group was sentenced to death, and they were blindfolded and led out to the scene of their public execution before a message arrived from the Tsar announcing their last-minute reprieve. One of the other men was said to have gone insane during this ordeal, whereas Dostoevsky endured it bravely.

He spent four years in a miserable Siberian prison camp, and in 1859 returned home to St. Petersburg. But Russia's most cultured city was in a prosperous, fashionably liberal mood, and the writer found himself unable to endure the vanity, false optimism and hypocrisy that now surrounded him.

He wrote "Notes From Underground", a fiercely satirical short novel, composed entirely of the fictional notes, confessions and rants left behind by a lonely St. Petersburg urbanite, a former middle-ranking civil servant in the Russian bureaucracy who now hid in the "underground" railing furiously against polite society while secretly yearning -- and comically failing -- to be accepted by it. In exquisitely self-contradictory language, rich with confusion and repressed emotion, Dostoevsky pinned his narrator to a board, a wriggling specimen of humanity, like a butterfly on display.

In October 1998, web designer and fiction writer Levi Asher released an exciting new digital movie based on the Dostoevsky novel, designed for viewing on a desktop computer (Windows or Mac) via CD-Rom. Asher's version of "Notes From Underground" updates the setting to modern-day New York City, and features a sensitive performance by Phil Zampino as the "Underground Man," a former middle-manager at a Wall Street bank who now wanders the streets of Manhattan looking for trouble.
